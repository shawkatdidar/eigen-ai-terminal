## Today I Learned

### Compute-for-Equity as Capital Structure

**Why this came up:** Anthropic's $5B additional Amazon investment + $100B AWS 10-year spend commitment + 5 GW Trainium2-4 allocation (April 20). Same structural pattern as the Amazon-OpenAI $50B deal from February 2026.

**The mechanism.** A frontier AI lab needs two scarce resources to ship a next-generation model: (1) capital (billions of dollars to pay for compute, researchers, data, hardware); (2) compute capacity (measured in gigawatts of secured datacenter power + chips + interconnect). Traditionally, labs raised equity capital from venture investors and separately signed compute contracts with cloud providers as a customer. Compute-for-equity merges these flows. The hyperscaler (AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft Azure) invests equity AND secures long-term compute allocation in a single contract. From the lab's side, this locks capacity for a decade — essential when compute capacity itself is the binding constraint. From the hyperscaler's side, the equity gives them upside on the lab's success AND guaranteed utilization of their silicon/datacenter spend. The contract becomes structurally equivalent to a long-term commercial real-estate lease where the tenant also owns part of the landlord.

**Why this matters now.** Three structural shifts compound:
1. **Training capex has inflated past venture-fund range.** A GPT-5/Claude-class training run is now priced in gigawatt-years of compute. Traditional venture rounds of $10–50B are insufficient; the only pools of capital at that scale are hyperscaler balance sheets.
2. **Compute capacity is physically constrained.** Power interconnects take years to permit; chip manufacturing has multi-year lead times. A lab without pre-committed GW-scale capacity cannot ship a 2027 frontier model, regardless of how much cash it raises.
3. **Exclusive-dealing is implicit.** Anthropic's $100B AWS commitment over 10 years means Anthropic's workload mix cannot rebalance to Google Cloud / Azure without breaking the contract structure. The "lab-neutral compute substrate" positioning (Google's announcement today, running Anthropic + TML + Gemini on the same GB300s) is the counter-narrative.

**The judgment framework.** When evaluating a frontier lab's 2026-2028 capacity story, the three questions that separate a credible roadmap from a narrative:

1. **Which hyperscaler is the lab's equity + compute primary?** If you cannot name one, the lab has not secured long-term capacity. Anthropic = AWS (now $13B invested, $100B 10-yr committed). OpenAI = Amazon ($50B Feb 2026) + SoftBank + Azure (legacy). Thinking Machines Lab = Google (multi-billion, announced April 22) + NVIDIA partnership. DeepSeek = Huawei + first-ever external VC ($300M).
2. **What silicon does the commit depend on, and is it shipping?** Anthropic's 5 GW commitment spans Trainium2 (shipping), Trainium3 (ramping), Trainium4 (not yet available). If AWS slips Trainium4, Anthropic's 2027 capacity plan slips with it. Silicon roadmap risk = lab capacity risk.
3. **What's the lab's fallback if the hyperscaler slips?** Anthropic has no structural fallback — the $100B commit is structurally exclusive. Google (TML) has non-exclusive language + NVIDIA partnership. DeepSeek has Huawei + external VC. The fallback question determines whether a lab's roadmap is fragile or resilient.

**Applied to today's signals:** The Anthropic-Amazon commit looks like victory for both sides today. But read through the frame: Anthropic is now structurally locked to Trainium roadmap execution. If AWS ships Trainium4 on time at the expected price/performance, the deal is a masterstroke. If AWS slips by 12 months, Anthropic is 12 months behind OpenAI's Stargate + Microsoft combined capacity. The single-point-of-failure risk did not exist under traditional equity + separate compute contracts — it is an emergent property of compute-for-equity structures.

### Benchmark Moats and Their Collapse

**Why this came up:** Moonshot Kimi K2.6 (open weights, 1T MoE) scored 58.6% on SWE-Bench Pro, beating GPT-5.4 (57.7%) and Claude Opus 4.6 (53.4%) at ~10× lower token cost.

**The mechanism.** A closed frontier model has three possible moats: (1) *weights moat* (the trained parameters are secret); (2) *benchmark moat* (the model reliably scores higher on benchmarks that drive procurement decisions); (3) *product moat* (integrations, UX, trust, reliability). Benchmark moats are the weakest of the three because they can be erased by any competing lab that spends enough compute on training signal targeting the same benchmark. Weights moats are stronger but still erode when a competitor releases open weights that match or beat on the same benchmarks — because then the weights-secret argument loses its customer-facing relevance.

**Why Kimi K2.6 matters.** Last window (April 17), Stanford AI Index 2026 documented that SWE-Bench Verified saturated from 60% to ~100% in twelve months — the benchmark itself had "expired as a discriminator." The community responded by shifting to SWE-Bench Pro, a harder, unsaturated variant. Kimi K2.6 beat closed frontier on SWE-Bench Pro — the new discriminator benchmark — at 10× lower token cost, with open weights, within weeks of the benchmark's adoption. The collapse cycle (new benchmark → closed-lab lead → open-weight parity) compressed from ~12 months (prior cycles) to ~30–60 days.

**The judgment framework.** When a procurement team asks "which model should we use for X" and the answer depends on benchmark scores:

1. **Is the benchmark saturated for the model class?** If frontier models score within 2pp of each other, benchmark scoring is noise. Other dimensions (price, latency, workflow fit) become the actual decision inputs.
2. **Is the benchmark reproducible in your workload?** Benchmarks are synthetic; your real workload is not. A 5pp benchmark gap that doesn't reproduce on your data is not a procurement differentiator.
3. **How fast does open-weight catch up?** Historically 9–12 months. Kimi K2.6 compressed it to weeks. If the open-weight catch-up time is shorter than your procurement cycle, the "closed is better" argument is fragile.

**Applied to today's signals:** Any procurement evaluation that chose Claude Opus 4.6 over an open alternative on the basis of SWE-Bench Pro score in February 2026 is now a decision driven by a moot fact. The correct framing for 2026-Q2 evaluation cycles is: "We chose Claude because of workflow + trust + enterprise controls, AND SWE-Bench Pro is now parity between open and closed, AND the cost ratio is 10×." That framing survives. "We chose Claude because it scored higher on SWE-Bench Pro" does not survive the scrutiny of a CFO running a 2026-H2 renewal review.

### Quick Glossary

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| HBM (High Bandwidth Memory) | Stacked DRAM on the same package as the compute die; delivers 5–10× higher memory bandwidth than conventional DRAM, critical for feeding parameters to compute during inference |
| SRAM (Static RAM) | Fastest memory tier on-chip, feeds compute units directly; measured per-core, usually in tens of MB |
| GW (gigawatt) | 1 billion watts; used as unit for datacenter-scale power commitments. 5 GW = power for a medium-sized city |
| Vera Rubin / NVL72 | NVIDIA's latest-gen platform; NVL72 links 72 GPUs into one coherent compute domain |
| Rubin | NVIDIA's GPU architecture generation following Blackwell; A5X is the bare-metal instance class |
| TPU (Tensor Processing Unit) | Google-designed AI accelerator; 8th-gen announced today as TPU 8t (training) + TPU 8i (inference) |
| Ironwood | Google's 7th-gen TPU (prior generation); TPU 8t claims 3× its training throughput |
| MoE (Mixture of Experts) | Architecture where only a fraction of total parameters activate per token; decouples model capacity (large) from inference cost (small) |
| ELO rating | Competitive head-to-head score; higher = wins more matchups against peer models |
| SI channel (Systems Integrator) | Traditional enterprise-software distribution path via consulting firms (Accenture, Deloitte, etc.) — customers buy through the SI, not direct from the vendor |
| Compute-for-equity | Frontier-lab capital structure where a hyperscaler invests equity AND secures long-term compute allocation in a single contract |
| SRT (Significant Risk Transfer) | Bank capital-relief instrument: bank offloads part of a loan portfolio's default risk to external investors in exchange for a premium |
| ILS (Insurance-Linked Security) | Financial instrument where investors take quota-share of an insurer's risk (e.g., catastrophe bonds); sidecar structure lets investors participate per-contract |
| VLA (Vision-Language-Action) | Model class for robotics: takes camera input + natural-language task, outputs motor actions |
| GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) | RL training method that skips the value network; computes advantages from relative rewards within a sampled group, reducing memory footprint vs standard PPO |
