## Today I Learned

### Model Composition: A New Pattern for AI Cost-Performance Optimization

**Why this came up:** Anthropic's Advisor Strategy release

When you want the best possible output from an AI system, the intuitive approach is to use the most capable (and expensive) model for everything. But Anthropic's Advisor Strategy demonstrates a fundamentally different pattern: **model composition** — where a cheaper "executor" model does most of the work and only consults a more expensive "advisor" model at critical decision points.

The results are counterintuitive: Sonnet 4.6 consulting Opus 4.6 at key moments scored 74.8% on SWE-bench Multilingual, versus 72.1% for Sonnet alone — a meaningful improvement. But it cost 11.9% *less* per task than running Sonnet solo in an agentic loop, because the advisor consultation (400-700 tokens) is far cheaper than the additional retries and dead-end explorations Sonnet would otherwise make without guidance.

This is a "macro-level MoE" pattern (Mixture of Experts — where only a fraction of the model's capacity activates per input). In a standard MoE architecture, a router selects 2-4 expert sub-networks per token. In the Advisor pattern, the "router" is the executor model itself, deciding when it needs help — and the "experts" are entire models at different capability tiers.

The key insight: the cost of being wrong on a hard problem (wasted retries, dead ends, cascading errors) often exceeds the cost of a brief consultation with a more capable model. This is why the cheaper approach is also the better-performing one.
